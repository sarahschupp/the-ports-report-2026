Strategic Framework, 2026 to 2027

The Four Forces Reshaping U.S. Gateway Strategy

These are not cyclical trends. They are structural shifts in supply-chain architecture that will determine which ports and which submarkets capture the next wave of institutional industrial demand. Each force is tracked weekly in The Ports Report with leading indicators and site-selection implications.

Force 01
Mexico Nearshoring Surge
Manufacturing migration from Asia to northern Mexico is accelerating along the Gulf Coast and inland corridors. As product routes consolidate through Houston, Laredo, and El Paso, Gulf port industrial markets are recording absorption levels not seen in a decade. This creates a narrow, time-limited window for tenants to secure sites ahead of the curve.
Leading Indicator
Houston industrial absorption +18% year-over-year; Class I rail carload growth through Eagle Pass at a 5-year high
Implication
Houston is the most available major gateway (5.1% vacancy) with FTZ 84 -- one of the largest in the U.S. -- offering inverted-tariff benefits on Mexican-manufactured goods
Action
Prioritize Houston and Gulf Coast submarkets for nearshoring-dependent operations before current availability window closes
Force 02
Infrastructure Velocity
Post-IIJA harbor deepening and rail intermodal investments are now delivering measurable throughput gains. Channel depth directly determines vessel class access: a 53-ft channel accommodates Ultra Large Container Vessels (ULCVs) at 18,000-plus TEU capacity, reducing per-TEU vessel cost by 12 to 18% versus Panamax-class calls. Ports that received capital in 2022 to 2024 are now widening their competitive advantage.
Leading Indicator
Savannah vessel-to-rail transfer now 22 hours (industry best-in-class); Charleston Leatherman Terminal adds 700K TEU annual capacity
Implication
Ports with completed deepening programs (Savannah 47 ft, Charleston 52 ft, Norfolk 55 ft) are pulling ahead on per-TEU economics and vessel schedule reliability
Action
Weight channel depth of 50 ft or greater heavily in gateway scoring models; sub-50 ft ports face growing vessel-class access constraints through 2027
Force 03
The Battery Belt
EV gigafactories in Georgia, Tennessee, and the Carolinas have created a new structural import corridor for cathode materials, battery cells, and EV components. Savannah and Charleston are emerging as the primary gateways for this trade flow, compressing industrial vacancy in their markets at a rate that outpaces all other coastal regions. This is a demand driver that will not reverse on a business cycle.
Leading Indicator
14-plus gigafactory announcements within 150 miles of Charleston and Savannah since 2022; Savannah asking rent up 38% over three years
Implication
Both markets now hold sub-3% industrial vacancy. New Class A bulk supply is being pre-leased before delivery, eliminating the traditional available-space window
Action
Secure Southeast sites in Q2 or Q3 2026; waiting for the market to signal scarcity will mean waiting 12 to 18 months longer than necessary
Force 04
Inventory Elasticity Collapse
Corporate supply chain officers have structurally shifted from just-in-time to just-in-case inventory management. Average safety stock targets are up 22% since 2021, permanently increasing demand for port-proximate industrial space. The companies moving fastest are locking in sites before the requirement fully materializes in their own planning cycles -- by which point the best sites are already off the market.
Leading Indicator
Six of eight tracked gateways hold sub-3.5% industrial vacancy; national bulk availability at a 15-year low; safety stock targets up 22% since 2021
Implication
Class A bulk sites near all major gateways are being absorbed before they reach the marketed-available stage. The traditional "look, then decide" search sequence is no longer viable
Action
Initiate site searches in Q2 to Q3 2026 for 2027 to 2028 occupancy. Peak-cycle escalation will compound costs significantly for mandates starting in late 2026
Framework updated weekly. Sources: AAPA Annual Report, CoStar Industrial Reports Q1 2026, USACE Harbor Performance Monitor, Georgia Quick Start program data, FMC Detention and Demurrage Study 2024.

Methodology and Data Sources

How The Ports Report is built and validated

Every data point in The Ports Report is sourced from a named, primary authority and updated on a defined cadence. The methodology below is provided for institutional clients and executive teams who require documented data provenance before incorporating intelligence into strategic decisions.

Primary Data Sources
TEU Volumes
American Association of Port Authorities (AAPA) Annual Statistics. Supplemented by monthly port authority cargo reports where available.
Industrial Vacancy and Rent
CoStar Industrial Availability Report, monthly. Class A bulk, 100K SF-plus, within 25 miles of port terminals.
Channel Depth
U.S. Army Corps of Engineers (USACE) Harbor Performance Monitor, 2024 Annual Summary. Cross-referenced against individual port authority published specifications.
FTZ Data
Foreign-Trade Zones Board Annual Report to Congress, FY2024. Zone boundaries and activation status cross-checked against NAFTZ member directory.
Drayage Cost Benchmarks
